    SCIENTIFIC PROJECTS AT UTFPR

    Impact of land use and soil texture on water retention in soils in the Amazon

    • Developed an undergraduate research project during the COVID-19 pandemic, working remotely with pre-collected experimental data on soil water retention in Amazonian forest and pasture areas.
    • Received processed laboratory datasets (undisturbed soil cores, water contents at multiple matric potentials, bulk density) from the advising professor due to field and lab restrictions during the pandemic period.
    • Fitted soil water retention curves using the RETC software and the van Genuchten model for 160 samples, generating representative curves by depth, land use (forest vs. pasture), and soil texture (sandy vs. clayey).
    • Analyzed changes in soil pore size distribution and available water storage associated with forest-to-pasture conversion under different textures, focusing on implications for soil structure, aeration, and plant-available water.
    • Interpreted and discussed results in the context of land-use change in the Amazon biome, highlighting how remote data analysis allowed the project to progress despite COVID-19 restrictions.

    Soil Physical limitations to soybean root elongation

    • Participated in an undergraduate research project during the COVID-19 pandemic focused on soil physical limitations to soybean root growth, working remotely with literature-based datasets rather than new field experiments.
    • Conducted a systematic literature review on the effects of soil physical constraints (mechanical impedance, water stress, and soil temperature) on soybean root elongation, following PRISMA guidelines for study selection.
    • Searched and screened articles in ScienceDirect, Web of Science, and Google Scholar using targeted keywords (e.g., root elongation, soybean, matric potential, soil penetration resistance, soil temperature), resulting in a final set of 8 core studies.
    • Extracted and organized quantitative data on root elongation rates under different soil physical conditions (temperature, penetration resistance, matric and osmotic potentials) for subsequent analysis and interpretation.
    • Helped interpret how increasing soil strength and decreasing water potential exponentially reduce soybean root elongation, and how optimal temperature ranges enhance root growth, with implications for no-till systems and soil compaction management.
    • Contributed to the scientific article "How do water, compaction and heat stresses affect soybean root elongation? A review".
